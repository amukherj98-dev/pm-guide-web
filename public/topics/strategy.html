Strategy & Continuous Improvement

Continuous improvement is the discipline of getting a little bit better, deliberately, on a regular basis. Not transformation - not reinvention. Just the conscious habit of asking, after every project or every cycle: what worked, what didn't, what could we do differently next time? And then acting on the answer.

The Strategic Context

A single project exists within an organisation's larger strategy. Before committing to a project, ask: does this advance the organisation's charitable purpose or business mission? Does it represent the best use of our limited capacity? Are we the right people to do this, or would referring it elsewhere better serve the beneficiaries? These questions connect the ECCSR framework - particularly the R (Results) and C (Creating) dimensions - to the organisation's long-term direction. See the Planning page for the business case structure that captures this thinking.

The PDCA Cycle - Deming's Most Portable Contribution

Plan → Do → Check → Act, repeated continuously:

  1. Plan - What change are we trying? What is the small experiment?
  2. Do - Try it on a small scale.
  3. Check - What actually happened? Compare with what you expected.
  4. Act - Adopt it, adapt it, or abandon it. Then start again.

For a charity running a service: try a new format for one drop-in (Plan), run it once (Do), ask staff and users what worked (Check), decide whether to roll it out or drop it (Act). The discipline is not dramatic. It is the habit of always running one small experiment and capturing the answer.

Lean's Seven Wastes - Translated for Office and Charity Work

Lean was originally about manufacturing. Translated for the work a small Irish org actually does:

  • Overproduction - producing reports nobody reads; building features nobody uses
  • Waiting - stalled approvals; "I'm waiting on Aoife to come back"
  • Transport - information moving through too many people; CC-on-CC-on-CC
  • Over-processing - five rounds of edits when two would do; ornate documentation for tiny projects
  • Inventory - a backlog of half-finished work; ten projects all 60% done
  • Motion - switching contexts constantly; people pulled between too many things
  • Defects - rework; mistakes caught late; double-handling

Walk through this list once a quarter with the team. The biggest wins almost always come from "waiting" (unblocking decisions) and "motion" (focusing on fewer things at a time).

DMAIC - A Structure for Recurring Problems

When something is going wrong and you cannot agree why, DMAIC provides a useful structure:

  1. Define - What exactly is the problem? Be specific.
  2. Measure - What is actually happening? Get data, not opinions.
  3. Analyse - Why is it happening? Root cause, not symptoms.
  4. Improve - What change will we try?
  5. Control - How will we make sure the improvement holds?

You do not need any Six Sigma certifications. The DMAIC discipline is free, and the order of the steps is the value - most teams want to skip from Define straight to Improve. Resist that.

A Six-Step Continuous Improvement Habit

  1. End every project with a retrospective - 60 minutes; three questions: what worked, what did not, what would we change. Write down the answers.
  2. Capture lessons in one place - a shared document; if the lesson is good but lost, it might as well not exist.
  3. At the start of every project, read the lessons - from this project type; from this team. Even five minutes.
  4. Pick one improvement per quarter - just one; make it real; do not try to fix everything.
  5. Make psychological safety routine - bad news welcomed; "I think this isn't working" is a contribution, not a complaint.
  6. Review the improvements once a year - what did we try? what stuck? what did not?

Common Pitfalls

  • Big-bang transformation - "we're going Lean!" followed by months of nothing; continuous improvement is small and cumulative by definition
  • No follow-through - retrospective happens; actions do not; worse than no retrospective
  • Improvement as blame - if retrospectives become "who broke it?", people stop being honest
  • Buying certifications instead of practising principles - a €3,000 Lean Six Sigma course for a five-person charity is not money well spent

Key Concepts

PDCA Cycle

Plan → Do → Check → Act, repeated continuously. The discipline of always running one small experiment and capturing the answer. Deming's most portable contribution to management practice.

Lean's Seven Wastes

Overproduction, Waiting, Transport, Over-processing, Inventory, Motion, Defects. Walk through the list quarterly with your team. The biggest wins usually come from "Waiting" and "Motion."

Retrospective

60 minutes after every project: what worked, what didn't, what would we change. Write the answers down. Read them before the next similar project. Without this, every project starts from scratch.

DMAIC

A structured problem-solving cycle for recurring issues: Define, Measure, Analyse, Improve, Control. The value is in the order - resist skipping from Define straight to Improve.
