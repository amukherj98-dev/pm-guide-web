Project Management Methodologies

A methodology is a defined way of running projects: processes, roles, terminology, and rules. The five names you should recognise are PMBOK, PRINCE2, Scrum, Kanban, and ISO 21502. You do not need to be certified in any of them to run good projects - but knowing what they are means you can speak the language when a funder, partner, or contractor brings them up.

Waterfall (Predictive)

Linear and sequential: requirements → design → build → test → deliver. Each stage finishes before the next begins. Strengths: predictable when requirements are stable; clear contract with funders ("this is exactly what you will get"); easy to plan budgets and milestones. Weaknesses: late discovery of misunderstood requirements; poor fit when conditions are uncertain; change requests are painful.

Use when: the deliverable is well-understood, requirements are stable, and the cost of change late in the project is high. For a small Irish org: a building refurb, a regulated compliance project, a printed publication.

Agile (Adaptive)

Iterative and incremental: deliver small pieces of value frequently, learn from each one, adjust. Born in the 2001 Agile Manifesto. Strengths: adapts well to changing requirements; surfaces problems early; engages stakeholders continuously. Weaknesses: hard to estimate total cost or end date up front; funder reporting can be awkward; easy to do badly (Agile theatre - daily stand-ups but no real iteration).

Use when: the deliverable will evolve and you have a stakeholder who can prioritise on the fly. For a small Irish org: developing a new service, designing a website, a campaign that responds to events.

Hybrid - What Most Small-Org Projects Actually Use

In practice, hybrid means: plan the shape of the project up front (timeline, big milestones, budget envelope, scope boundary), then run agile-flavoured cycles inside. "Water-Scrum-Fall" is the most common hybrid pattern: waterfall planning → scrum execution → waterfall release. The 2024 PMI Pulse of the Profession shows hybrid use at 31.5% of all projects globally and rising.

Use when: you have funder commitments that need fixed milestones, but the work itself is exploratory. This describes most small-org projects.

PMBOK 7 - Twelve Principles as a Sense-Check

PMI's 7th edition (2021) shifted from process-heavy to principle-based. The twelve Project Delivery Principles are worth knowing as a checklist for any project: Stewardship, Team, Stakeholders, Value, Systems thinking, Leadership, Tailoring, Quality, Complexity, Risk, Adaptability/resiliency, Change. For a small org, run through these as a sense-check on any project. They map closely onto Houghton's ECCSR framework.

PRINCE2 7 - Business Case at the Centre

Originally a UK government method, now widely used in the public and NGO sector. The principle of "continued business justification" means: if the business case fails at any point, kill the project. Useful when working with a public-sector partner that wants formal stage gates and exception reports. The 7th edition (2023) adds an explicit people focus and integrates sustainability. Heavy for a three-person charity team but worth recognising.

Scrum - The Agile Framework You Will Actually Meet

The most widely used Agile framework. Roles: Product Owner (owns what to build and in what order), Scrum Master (owns the process, removes blockers), Developers (the people doing the work). Events: Sprint (typically 2 weeks), Sprint Planning, Daily Stand-up (15 minutes: what I did / what I'll do / what's blocking me), Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective. For a small Irish charity team running a website refresh or a service redesign, a stripped-down Scrum (two-week sprints, one weekly stand-up, monthly retrospective) often works very well.

Kanban - Visual Flow Management

From Toyota. Less ceremony than Scrum: visualise the work on a board (To Do / Doing / Done), limit work in progress (a hard cap on how many items can be "Doing" at once), manage flow (focus on getting cards moving), make policies explicit. Kanban suits continuous ongoing work with no fixed end (helpdesk, content calendar, ongoing fundraising) and very small teams where Scrum's ceremonies feel like overkill. A free Trello board does the job.

Choosing Your Approach

A simple guide for a small Irish organisation:

  • Building / refurb / capital works → Waterfall
  • Compliance / governance project → Waterfall
  • Annual fundraising event → Hybrid (fixed date, content flexes)
  • New service design → Hybrid leaning Agile
  • Website or app development → Agile (Scrum or Kanban)
  • Ongoing service improvement → Kanban
  • Multi-year strategy delivery → Hybrid (annual waterfall, quarterly agile)

The key is tailoring - adapting the approach to fit the project - which is itself one of PMBOK 7's twelve principles.

Common Pitfalls

  • Cargo-cult Agile - daily stand-ups and Post-Its on the wall do not make you Agile
  • Cargo-cult Waterfall - a 50-page project plan nobody reads after week two
  • Religious wars - "Agile is better than Waterfall." They are tools. Match to the work.
  • Too much process for the project size - a €2,000 project does not need PRINCE2
  • Believing certification equals competence

Key Concepts

Waterfall vs Agile

Waterfall: plan everything upfront, sequential delivery, best for stable requirements. Agile: short iterative cycles, frequent feedback, best for evolving requirements. Most real projects are hybrid.

Scrum

The most common Agile framework. Roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers), time-boxed sprints (usually 2 weeks), five events (Planning, Daily Stand-up, Review, Retrospective). Strips well for small teams.

PMBOK 7 Principles

Twelve Project Delivery Principles (not processes): stewardship, team, stakeholders, value, systems thinking, leadership, tailoring, quality, complexity, risk, adaptability, change. A useful sense-check for any project.

Tailoring

Adapting the chosen approach to fit the actual project and team. A PMBOK 7 principle in its own right. No methodology should be applied unchanged to every project.
