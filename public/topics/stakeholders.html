Stakeholder Engagement

A stakeholder is anyone affected by your project, anyone who can affect it, or anyone who thinks they have a stake - whether you agree or not. The crucial distinction is between engagement (reciprocal - listening, building relationship) and management (unilateral - controlling, defending). For a small Irish charity, engagement is the only mode that fits.

Who Is a Stakeholder?

Stakeholders include everyone affected by the project, everyone who can affect it, and everyone who believes they have a stake - whether or not you agree with that belief. That third category is larger than organisations expect, and ignoring it creates problems later. For Irish charities, the default stakeholder list typically includes: your board and trustees, the Charities Regulator, your funders (Pobal, government departments, philanthropic trusts, private donors), your beneficiaries, volunteers and staff, partner organisations, and local authority or community stakeholders if the work is place-based.

Why Engagement Matters More for Small Organisations

A small Irish charity runs on legitimacy and trust. You do not have a marketing department, lobbyists, or a PR firm. You have your relationships - and those are won or lost project by project. Three specific reasons engagement matters more for you than for a corporate:

  • You do not have slack to recover from bad relationships. A funder who feels ignored may not give you a second chance.
  • Your beneficiaries are often your reason for existing. Treating them as "users to be managed" corrodes the mission.
  • Volunteers walk if they feel unheard. They are not paid. The only reason they stay is that they feel part of something.

Step 1 - Identify Everyone

Brainstorm widely. Useful prompts: Who pays for this? Who delivers it? Who benefits? Who could block it? Who could be affected unintentionally? Whose voice is usually missed - marginalised beneficiaries, silent funders, future users? Do not filter at this stage. Capture everyone, then prioritise.

Step 2 - Map on Influence × Interest

Plot each stakeholder on a 2×2 grid. High influence + high interest → Manage closely (your sponsors, key funders, lead beneficiaries - frequent two-way communication). High influence + low interest → Keep satisfied (senior people who will react if something goes badly wrong). Low influence + high interest → Keep informed (engaged supporters, most beneficiaries). Low influence + low interest → Monitor (wider community, observers). The "monitor" quadrant is the one you will most often regret ignoring.

Step 3 - Understand Why You Are Engaging

Kujala et al. (2022) describe three motivations for stakeholder engagement - moral, strategic, and pragmatic - and healthy engagement balances all three. Moral: this stakeholder has the right to be heard. Strategic: protecting or creating value for the project. Pragmatic: solving a specific problem early.

A red flag is engagement done purely for strategic reasons. That is the shortcut to tick-box consultation that beneficiaries see through immediately.

Step 4 - Plan Your Activities

For each significant stakeholder or group, decide: what they need to know; what you need from them; which channel to use (face-to-face, email, group forum, public meeting, newsletter); how often; and who on your team holds the relationship.

The Dark Side of Engagement

Kujala et al. flag patterns that look like engagement but are not - and for a charity these are reputationally lethal:

  • Tick-box consultation - running the workshop, then ignoring the input
  • Power imbalance - hearing the funder loudly but the beneficiary faintly
  • Bad-faith engagement - going through the motions to pacify rather than genuinely listen
  • Marginalising the inconvenient - quietly excluding stakeholders whose voice would be uncomfortable

If anyone in your team can identify any of these on a current project, surface it now rather than after a beneficiary calls it out publicly.

Common Pitfalls

  • Treating "the community" as one stakeholder - communities are heterogeneous
  • Confusing communication with engagement - a newsletter is not engagement
  • Engaging only at the start and end - the middle is where it really matters
  • Forgetting your own team - staff and volunteers are stakeholders too
  • Engagement fatigue - if you ask the same beneficiaries the same questions for five different projects, they will stop turning up

Key Concepts

Influence × Interest Grid

Maps stakeholders into four quadrants (manage closely / keep satisfied / keep informed / monitor) to focus communication effort where it creates most value.

Moral / Strategic / Pragmatic

Kujala et al.'s three-part motivation for engagement. Healthy engagement balances all three. Purely strategic engagement becomes tick-box consultation.

Dark Side of Engagement

Patterns that look like engagement but aren't: tick-box consultation, power imbalance, bad-faith engagement, marginalising inconvenient voices.

Stakeholder Communication Plan

A simple table matching each stakeholder to a channel, frequency, what they need, what you need from them, and a named owner.
