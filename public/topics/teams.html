Building Project Teams

Projects are delivered by people, not plans. The four enabling conditions identified by Hackman and refined by Haas & Mortensen - compelling direction, strong structure, supportive context, and shared mindset - distinguish teams that thrive from those that struggle.

Why Teams Fail in Small Organisations

In a small Irish charity or business, the project team is rarely a dedicated group. It is usually people doing the project alongside their normal duties - permanent staff with day-jobs, volunteers who can't be compelled, contractors working part-time, and board members who occasionally drop in with strong opinions. The traps are specific:

  • Heroic individualism - one person doing everything because asking for help feels harder than just doing it
  • Role blur - nobody is quite sure who is responsible for what, so things fall through the gaps
  • Engagement gap - the leader keeps pulling while the team waits for direction
  • Volunteer attrition - people disengage when the work feels pointless or the environment feels chaotic

ECCSR's second C - Collaborative - is the direct response to these traps. See the Collaboration page for the full framework.

Building the Team: Practical Steps

Define roles in writing

For every team member - including volunteers - write down what they are responsible for, what decisions they can make, and who they report to. Even a one-paragraph role description eliminates most of the conflict that emerges from ambiguity. "Could you help with X?" is much weaker than "Could you take responsibility for X each Tuesday morning?"

Agree norms of conduct

At the kick-off, spend 20 minutes agreeing how the team will work together: when and how you communicate, how disagreement is handled, what is confidential, what "done" looks like for key tasks. Written norms are not bureaucracy - they are the team's operating agreement.

Keep it small

Hackman's rule of thumb: under ten people. For most small-org projects, three to five is genuinely enough. Larger groups spend more time coordinating than working. If the scope requires more people, break into sub-teams with clear interfaces.

Build the shared mindset

Diverse, dispersed teams - the reality for most Irish charities - suffer from "us vs them" thinking and from information gaps that fill with assumption. Counter this with: a one-line objective everyone can repeat, regular check-ins that include five minutes of personal connection (not just task status), and deliberate perspective-taking ("what does this look like from the volunteer's point of view? from the funder's?").

Managing Volunteers Effectively

Volunteers are the backbone of most Irish charities, and they require a distinct approach. The key differences from managing employees:

  • Motivation is intrinsic - connect every task to the mission or the volunteer will not come back
  • Time is a gift - respect it absolutely; start on time, end on time, do not waste it on unnecessary meetings
  • Recognition is your main currency - public acknowledgement at board meetings, in newsletters, in person
  • Clarity prevents attrition - vague roles lead to disengagement; specific responsibilities lead to ownership
  • Exit ramps matter - make it easy for a volunteer to step back gracefully when life changes; burning the relationship loses a future ally

Running Effective Team Check-Ins

A good weekly project check-in is 15 minutes, same time each week, same structure: traffic-light status (green / amber / red) for each workstream; one-line update per workstream; anything blocking; new risks this week; decisions needed; actions assigned with owner and date. The rule: any red item gets discussed as long as it needs. Green items get one sentence. Amber is when you intervene - do not let it drift to red unnoticed.

Common Pitfalls

  • No clear roles - people step on each other or leave gaps
  • Too many people - coordination overhead outweighs contribution
  • Rewarding the loud, ignoring the steady - the quiet reliable volunteer is your most valuable asset
  • Skipping retrospectives - without them, the same mistakes repeat on every project
  • Confusing pace with progress - a busy team is not necessarily a delivering team

Key Concepts

Role Clarity

Every team member (including volunteers) needs a written description of what they are responsible for and what decisions they can make. Ambiguity is the enemy of collaboration.

Team Norms

Agreements made at kick-off about how the team works together: communication channels, decision rights, handling disagreement, definition of done. Written norms prevent later conflict.

Volunteer Management

Motivation is intrinsic; time is a gift; recognition is your main currency; clarity prevents attrition; exit ramps preserve future relationships. Volunteers cannot be managed like employees.

Weekly Check-In

15 minutes, traffic-light status, one-line per workstream, blockers, new risks, decisions, actions with owners. Red items get full attention; green items get one sentence.
