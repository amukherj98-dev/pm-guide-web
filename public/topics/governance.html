Project Governance

Governance is the system by which a project is directed and controlled. It answers: who has the authority to make which decisions, how are those decisions made, and who is accountable for the outcomes? For Irish charities, this question cannot be separated from the Charities Governance Code - which the Charities Regulator uses to assess organisational health.

What Project Governance Covers

Governance is not the same as management. Management is about running the project. Governance is about ensuring the right oversight structures exist so that the right decisions are made by the right people at the right time. In a small Irish charity or business, the two are often done by overlapping groups of people - which makes clarity about roles even more important, not less.

Project governance covers: the project sponsor and their responsibilities; the project manager and their decision rights; the role of the board or steering group; escalation paths (when does the PM escalate to the sponsor, and when does the sponsor escalate to the board?); reporting requirements; and how conflicts of interest are managed.

The Project Sponsor

The sponsor is the person ultimately accountable for the project - typically a senior leader, board member, or CEO for a charity. The sponsor is not the person running the project day-to-day; that is the project manager's role. The sponsor's job is to provide air cover (protecting the project from organisational interference), remove blockers the PM cannot remove, make decisions that are above the PM's delegated authority, and formally sign off on the project charter and closure.

Having a committed, accessible sponsor is one of the strongest predictors of project success. A sponsor who is too busy to engage is a governance failure waiting to happen.

Decision Rights and Escalation

Every project needs agreed decision rights: what the project manager can decide independently, what requires sponsor sign-off, and what requires board approval. For a small Irish charity, a simple three-level model works well:

  • PM level - day-to-day execution decisions within the agreed scope and budget
  • Sponsor level - scope changes, budget variances beyond tolerance, key personnel changes
  • Board level - major changes to purpose or budget, risk exposures above a defined threshold, project termination

PRINCE2 calls the PM-to-sponsor trigger an "exception" - when something happens that takes the project outside agreed tolerances, the PM reports an exception and the sponsor decides how to respond.

Board Oversight of Projects

The Charities Governance Code (Principle 1 - Advancing Charitable Purpose; Principle 5 - Being Accountable and Transparent) places a clear obligation on boards to oversee how charitable resources are used. In practice this means: the board should receive regular project updates (not as raw detail, but as a brief RAG-status summary); the board should be informed of significant risks and any scope or budget changes beyond agreed tolerances; and the board should formally receive and acknowledge the final project closure report.

The most common governance failure in small charities is not deliberate - it is the informal drift where a committed staff member or volunteer leads a project without ever formally reporting to the board, and the board discovers what happened only when something goes wrong.

Conflict of Interest in Projects

Conflicts of interest are especially acute in small organisations where people wear multiple hats. A board member who is also a supplier, a volunteer who is a relative of a contractor, a project manager evaluating a tender from a former employer. The appropriate response is always transparency and recusal - declare the interest in writing, step back from the relevant decision, and document the fact that recusal occurred. The Charities Governance Code expects annual conflict-of-interest declarations for all board members and senior staff.

Reporting and Transparency

Good project governance requires honest reporting. This means: RAG (Red/Amber/Green) status that reflects reality, not wishful thinking; honest reporting of variances against budget and schedule; surfacing risks before they become issues; and acknowledging what has not gone to plan in final reports. Funders and boards can handle honest news; what destroys relationships is discovering that a problem was known and not reported.

Common Pitfalls

  • No named sponsor - a project without an accountable senior leader has no governance
  • Sponsor too busy to engage - creates a vacuum that usually gets filled with poor decisions
  • RAG status inflation - always green until suddenly red; amber is not a problem, it is an early warning
  • Board bypassed on major decisions - creates legal and reputational risk for the charity
  • Undeclared conflicts of interest - the most common single source of reputational damage in Irish charities

Key Concepts

Project Sponsor

The person ultimately accountable for the project. Provides air cover, removes blockers, makes decisions above the PM's authority, signs off on charter and closure. Sponsor engagement is one of the strongest predictors of project success.

Decision Rights

A three-level model: PM decides day-to-day execution; Sponsor decides scope/budget changes; Board decides major changes and termination. Write it down before work begins.

Charities Governance Code

Principles 1 and 5 require boards to oversee how charitable resources are used. Regular project updates, risk reporting, and formal closure reports are governance requirements, not optional.

Conflict of Interest

Declare in writing, recuse from the relevant decision, document the recusal. Annual declarations for all board members and senior staff. Undeclared conflicts are the most common source of reputational damage in Irish charities.
