Risk Management

A risk is a potential, negative consequence event. Risk management is the discipline of foreseeing those problems in advance and deciding how you will respond - before the phone call comes with bad news.

What is a Project Risk?

In Joe Houghton's plain definition: a risk is "a potential, negative consequence event." Picture a phone call in the future delivering bad news - something has gone wrong and serious consequences have followed. That is a risk that has materialised. Risk management is the discipline of foreseeing those calls and doing something about them before they happen.

For small organisations in Ireland, risk management does not need to be complex or bureaucratic. Even a simple one-page risk register, reviewed at every project meeting, makes a real difference. The key is making risk thinking a regular habit, not a one-off exercise at the start of a project.

Why Small Organisations Are Especially Vulnerable

Small charities and businesses are disproportionately exposed to risk because:

  • One key person leaving can cripple a project
  • Cash flow is tighter - there is less room to absorb a financial hit
  • Reputation damage is harder to recover from
  • Funder confidence, once lost, is hard to rebuild
  • Volunteers cannot be compelled to stay when things get difficult

The good news: the cost of doing basic risk management well is far lower than the cost of doing it badly or not at all.

Identifying Risks

Brainstorm with the whole team - do not filter at this stage. Useful prompts: What could go wrong with the budget? With the timeline? With the people involved? With suppliers or partners? What external factors (regulation, weather, economy) could affect us? What are we depending on that we do not control?

Use a Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) to group risks by type: Financial, Operational, Legal, Reputational, Health & Safety, Technology, Stakeholder. This helps spot gaps and assign the right people to investigate each category.

Classifying Each Risk

Score every risk on two dimensions - Probability (1–5, where 5 = certain) and Impact (1–5, where 5 = severe) - and multiply them to get a risk score. A score of 25 demands immediate attention. A score of 1 can probably be ignored. The interesting decisions live in the middle, which is where judgement matters.

Ask for each risk: worst case and typical case? Effect on timescale, budget, scope, performance, and team safety?

The Risk Register

A simple spreadsheet with these columns is enough for most small projects: Risk ID, Description, Probability, Impact, Score, Stage, Detection trigger, Effect, Response strategy, Action, Owner, Status. Keep it short - a ten-row register reviewed weekly is worth ten times a hundred-row register that nobody opens.

Five Response Strategies

  • Prevention - Stop it happening or negate its impact (e.g., upgrade systems before the breach, not after)
  • Reduction - Reduce likelihood or limit impact to acceptable levels (e.g., cross-train two people on critical tasks)
  • Transference - Move the financial consequence to a third party through insurance or contract (the risk itself does not disappear)
  • Contingency - Have a plan ready if it does happen (backup venue booked, emergency fund set aside)
  • Acceptance - Decide consciously that no action is warranted. Never accidentally.

The Cultural Dimension

The Challenger disaster in 1986 is Houghton's teaching example. Engineers at Morton Thiokol had identified the O-ring failure risk in cold weather and warned management repeatedly. The risk was known - but management overruled technical concerns under schedule pressure, and seven astronauts died.

The lesson is not about engineering. It is about how risk management fails: risks identified but not acted on; voice without authority; delivery pressure overriding safety concerns. For a small Irish charity, the equivalent is a volunteer flagging a safeguarding concern that gets brushed aside because "we need this event to go ahead." The discipline of risk management is partly technical - register, scoring, response planning. The bigger half is cultural: do people feel safe raising risks? Are risks taken seriously when raised? Are they tracked over time?

Common Pitfalls

  • Identifying risks once at the start and never revisiting - they evolve
  • Listing risks without assigning owners - without an owner, nothing happens
  • Confusing risks (might happen) with issues (are happening now)
  • Producing a risk register for a funder and never looking at it again
  • Over-engineering - a 100-row register for a €5,000 project

Key Concepts

Risk Register

A living document recording all identified risks with probability, impact score, owner, and response. A ten-row register reviewed weekly beats a hundred-row register nobody reads.

Probability × Impact Matrix

Score each risk on likelihood (1–5) and impact (1–5) and multiply. Focus energy on high-scoring risks; consciously accept or monitor the low ones.

Five Response Strategies

Prevention, Reduction, Transference, Contingency, Acceptance. Choose one for every significant risk. Acceptance is valid - but must be a deliberate decision, not an oversight.

Risk Breakdown Structure

Group risks by category (Financial, Operational, Legal, Reputational, H&S, Technology, Stakeholder) to ensure no area is missed and to assign the right people to each category.
