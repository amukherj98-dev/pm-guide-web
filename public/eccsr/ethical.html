ECCSR Framework

Ethical

The E in ECCSR. Ethics is not a compliance exercise - it is the foundation on which trust is built. For a small Irish charity, public trust is your operating capital. Once it is lost, it does not come back fast. The PMI Code of Ethics identifies four pillars: Responsibility, Respect, Fairness, and Honesty.

Ethics as a Foundation

The E in ECCSR sits first for a reason. Houghton's framework places ethics at the foundation of every project decision. Ethical project management means being honest about progress and problems, treating all contributors and beneficiaries with respect, using resources for their intended purposes, and making decisions that reflect the organisation's values even when it is inconvenient to do so.

For small businesses and charities in Ireland, ethical practice is especially visible. Organisations are embedded in communities. Staff, volunteers, and service users know each other. Reputational damage from perceived unfairness or dishonesty travels quickly and is difficult to repair.

Responsibility

Own your decisions, actions, and the consequences - including the decisions you fail to make. Inform yourself of the laws governing your work (Charities Act 2009, GDPR, employment law). Report unethical or illegal conduct. Do not accept work you cannot deliver. Own mistakes early - concealing a problem to "fix it quietly" almost always makes it worse.

A useful test: would you be comfortable if this decision were on the front page of the Irish Times tomorrow? If not, that is information.

Respect

For a charity, respect extends beyond colleagues. Beneficiaries deserve dignity in language, in process, and in how their stories are used - photo consent, the right to refuse to be a "case study," the right to anonymity. Volunteers are giving time, not labour. The donor who gave €50 deserves the same care as the funder who gave €50,000.

Fairness

Transparent procurement (three quotes for anything significant; document why you chose what you chose; avoid sole-sourcing to a friend's company). Open recruitment. Declared conflicts of interest, updated annually, with recusal from relevant decisions. Clear and visible criteria for who gets help when demand exceeds supply.

Honesty

Never overstate impact in funder reports. If a target was missed, say so and explain why - funders respect this; they do not respect a report that mysteriously hits 100% every quarter. A project status that has been "on track" for six weeks while everyone privately knows it is not will eventually become a crisis. Tell the truth early. Restricted funds stay restricted.

Building a Bottom-Up Ethical Culture

Hess (2014) argues that ethics cannot be built top-down alone. Frontline staff and volunteers need voice (a safe way to raise concerns), recognition (for ethical behaviour, not just performance), and protection (from retaliation when they speak up). For a small Irish charity, this maps onto the Charities Governance Code's expectation of a written whistleblowing policy and meaningful stakeholder engagement with beneficiaries.

Key Concepts

Responsibility

Own your decisions and their consequences. Report problems early. Do not accept work you cannot deliver. Know the law that governs your work.

Respect

Beneficiaries deserve dignity in language, process, and stories. Volunteers give time, not labour. Every donor deserves equal care regardless of amount.

Fairness

Transparent procurement, open recruitment, declared conflicts of interest, clear and visible service allocation criteria. Fairness is also about not playing favourites.

Honesty

Never overstate impact. Tell the truth about project status. Restricted funds stay restricted. Use beneficiary stories only with consent.
