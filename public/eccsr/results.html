ECCSR Framework

Results

The R in ECCSR closes the loop. Without results, the other four dimensions do not matter. But results does not just mean "we got it done." It means: did this project actually deliver the value we set out to create - honestly measured, honestly reported?

What Results Means in ECCSR

The R in ECCSR is the accountability dimension. It asks, at the end (and throughout): did we deliver the value we set out to create? Results should be three things: measurable (defined before you start, not reverse-engineered at the end), meaningful (connected to your organisation's mission, not just to activity), and honestly reported (including what went wrong and what you would do differently).

ECCSR's R is also about closing properly - capturing lessons, recognising contributions, and feeding learning back into the next project. A project that ends without a retrospective loses its most valuable asset: the knowledge of what happened and why.

Defining Results Before You Start

The single most important thing you can do to improve project results is to define what success looks like before the project begins. Not "we will improve community engagement" - but "by December, 50 new community members will have attended at least three events, and 20 will be engaged in the steering group." The specificity is what makes it measurable. Vague objectives produce vague outcomes and make honest evaluation impossible.

For a charity reporting to funders, this matters practically as well as philosophically. A funder who reads "community engagement improved" in a final report learns nothing. A funder who reads "52 new members attended at least three events (against a target of 50); 18 joined the steering group (against a target of 20)" trusts the organisation to deliver and report accurately.

Monitoring Results Through the Project

Results are not just measured at the end - they are tracked throughout. A good monitoring approach: define the two or three key metrics that matter most for this project; review them at every project status meeting; surface variances honestly ("we are behind on X; here is why and here is the plan"); and adjust the approach if the evidence suggests what you are doing is not working.

The traffic-light (RAG) system is a useful shortcut: Green = on track; Amber = slippage or risk emerging; Red = needs a decision today. Amber is not a problem - it is an early warning. The failure is allowing amber to drift unnoticed to red.

Honest Reporting

Honest reporting is the hardest dimension of Results in practice. The temptation is to frame everything positively - to write reports that show the project in the best possible light. The problem is that this destroys the value of reporting. If a funder or board member cannot trust a report to tell them what really happened, the report is useless.

Specific habits: if a target was missed, say so and explain why. If something you planned turned out not to work, say so and describe what you learned. If the project is behind schedule, say so and explain what you are doing about it. Funders and boards can handle honest news; what they cannot recover from is discovering that a problem was known and concealed.

Closure and Lessons Learned

ECCSR's R includes closing properly. A project that ends without formal closure has wasted its own experience. Closure means: confirming all deliverables are complete (or formally varied); reconciling the final budget; submitting the final funder report; thanking volunteers and staff publicly and personally; and - most importantly - running a retrospective and capturing the lessons in a place where they can be found before the next similar project begins.

Key Concepts

Measurable, Meaningful, Honest

Results must be defined before the project starts (not reverse-engineered at the end), connected to the organisational mission, and reported honestly - including what went wrong.

RAG Monitoring

Green (on track), Amber (slippage or risk emerging), Red (needs decision today). Amber is an early warning, not a problem. The failure is letting amber drift to red without intervention.

Honest Reporting

If a target was missed, say so and explain why. If something did not work, say so and describe what you learned. Funders and boards can handle honest news; they cannot recover from discovering concealment.

Project Closure

Confirm all deliverables, reconcile the budget, submit the final report, thank contributors, run a retrospective, capture lessons. A project that ends without formal closure wastes its own experience.
