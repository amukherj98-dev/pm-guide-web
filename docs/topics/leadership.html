Leadership in Project Management

A project doesn't deliver itself; people do. Leadership in project management is the work of getting a group of people - often with different roles, skills, and motivations - pulling in the same direction long enough to deliver something useful. For a small Irish organisation, this means earning the right to be followed, not commanding it.

Three Leadership Styles in the Toolkit

Houghton (2023, ch.13) presents three leadership styles that show up most in project work. The answer for most project managers is never "pick one" - it is "have all three available."

Transformational Leadership

Inspiring through vision and purpose; lifting people to a higher standard. Use this when the project needs energy and belief - at launch, during a crisis, at the end of a difficult stretch. Tell the story. Remind everyone why this matters.

Situational Leadership

Adapting your style to the person and the moment (Hersey & Blanchard's model). Use a directive style with a new volunteer who needs to know exactly what to do. A coaching style with a capable staff member growing into a new role. A delegating style with a senior colleague who knows more than you do.

Servant Leadership

The underlying default. Your job as project manager is to make the team able to do their job. Remove blockers. Get them what they need. Give credit; absorb blame. The most capable teams are those whose leader quietly made everything easier.

The Four Enabling Conditions (Haas & Mortensen)

Haas & Mortensen (2016) identify four conditions that distinguish teams that thrive from those that struggle - especially as teams become more diverse, dispersed, digital, and dynamic. These map directly onto the reality of most Irish charity teams.

1. Compelling Direction

The team needs a purpose that is clear (everyone knows what success looks like), challenging (energising, not trivial), and consequential (it matters). "Improve outcomes for our community" is a mission. "By December, run the new drop-in service three days a week with at least 30 unique users a month" is a project direction. Tie individual roles to the objective explicitly. Repeat it - purpose erodes faster than you expect.

2. Strong Structure

The right people, the right number, the right roles, the right norms. Diversity of skills and experience matters - avoid teams who all think alike. Hackman's rule of thumb: under ten people. For a small charity, three to five is often enough. Agree norms of conduct at the start: meeting times, decision rights, how disagreement is handled, what is confidential. For mixed staff and volunteer teams, explicit role clarity in writing is essential.

3. Supportive Context

Teams need information (access to what they need), resources (tools, training, time), and recognition (for team performance, not just individual). Small organisations often struggle on resources - the budget is tight, the training budget tighter. Two practical responses: spend a small amount well rather than nothing at all. Make recognition free and frequent - a genuine thank-you and a public mention at the next board meeting cost nothing and matter.

4. Shared Mindset

The biggest challenge for dispersed teams. Two enemies: "us vs them" thinking between staff and volunteers, operations and the board, this team and another department; and incomplete information that fills with assumption. Build common identity. Encourage perspective-taking - actively understand others' contexts. Use structured communication routines - five minutes of personal connection at the start of a meeting is not wasted time.

Leading Volunteers

Volunteers are not employees and the rules are different. Motivation must come from purpose - you cannot compel; you can only inspire. Make the work feel worthwhile: framing three hours of envelope-stuffing as "we sent 400 letters reaching families this week" is different work from the same task described as administrative. Respect their time absolutely - start meetings on time, end on time, do not pad. Provide clear roles. Recognise publicly. And have an exit ramp - make it easy to step back without burning the relationship.

Self-Assessment for Your Team

Periodically - every couple of months on a longer project - check three things: Are we energised? (Engagement.) Are we learning? (Development.) Are we delivering? (Performance.) If the answer to any of these is "no" for an extended period, intervene. Honesty here is more valuable than optimism.

Common Pitfalls

  • Conflating leadership with seniority - the most senior person is not always the right project leader
  • Hero PM - trying to do everything yourself; the team disengages because there is no real role left for them
  • Avoiding hard conversations - underperformance addressed early is manageable; left, it corrodes the team
  • Over-meeting - three calls a week for alignment, leaving less time for actual work
  • Rewarding the loud, ignoring the steady - the volunteer who comes every week without fanfare is your most valuable asset

Key Concepts

Three Leadership Styles

Transformational (vision and inspiration), Situational (adapt to person and moment), Servant (remove blockers, give credit). The skilled project manager moves between all three deliberately.

Compelling Direction

Purpose that is clear, challenging, and consequential. Translate the mission into a specific, measurable project objective. Repeat it - purpose erodes faster than expected.

Strong Structure

Right people, right size (under 10), right roles, agreed norms of conduct. Written role clarity is non-negotiable in mixed staff-and-volunteer teams.

Servant Leadership

The underlying default for all project managers. Your job is to make the team able to do theirs. Remove blockers. Give credit. Absorb blame.
