Ethics in Project Management

Ethics in project management is the practice of doing the right thing under pressure - when the easier thing would be to shade the truth, cut the corner, or look the other way. For a charity especially, ethics is not a compliance exercise. It is the foundation on which public trust is built.

The PMI Four Pillars

Houghton's framework draws directly on the PMI Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct, built around four core values:

  • Responsibility - owning your decisions, actions, and the consequences
  • Respect - for people, resources, the environment, and the work itself
  • Fairness - making decisions impartially, free from competing self-interest
  • Honesty - communicating truthfully in conduct and content

These are not aspirational nice-to-haves. For a small Irish charity, the stakes are existential. Public trust is your operating capital. Restricted funds are not yours to repurpose. The Charities Regulator can investigate, sanction, or remove your registration. And volunteers watch how decisions are made - if they see corners cut, they leave, or worse, they learn to cut their own.

Pillar 1 - Responsibility

Responsibility means owning the decisions you make or fail to make. In practice: inform yourself of the laws and rules that govern your work (Charities Act 2009, GDPR, employment law, sector-specific rules). Report unethical or illegal conduct to the appropriate body - internally first if safe, externally if needed. Do not accept work you cannot deliver. Own mistakes early - concealing a problem to "fix it quietly" almost always makes it worse.

A useful test: would you be comfortable if this decision were on the front page of the Irish Times tomorrow? If the answer is no, that is information.

Pillar 2 - Respect

For a charity, respect extends well beyond colleagues. Beneficiaries deserve dignity - in language, in process, and in stories told about them. Photo consent, the right to refuse to be a "case study," the right to anonymity. Volunteers are giving time, not labour - treat them as collaborators, not unpaid staff. Resources matter - the donor who gave €50 deserves the same care as the funder who gave €50,000.

Pillar 3 - Fairness

The high-risk areas are procurement (run a fair process; three quotes for anything significant; document why you chose what you chose; avoid sole-sourcing to a friend's company), recruitment (open competitions, declared interests, structured interviews), conflict of interest declarations (routine, in writing, updated annually, recused from relevant decisions), and service allocation (criteria for who gets help must be clear, fair, and visible).

Pillar 4 - Honesty

The hardest in practice. Specific habits: never overstate impact in funder reports - if a target was missed, say so and explain why. A project status that has been "on track" for six weeks while everyone privately knows it is not will eventually become a crisis; tell the truth early. Do not use beneficiary stories without consent. Restricted funds must be spent on the purpose for which they were given.

Building a Bottom-Up, Outside-In Ethical Culture

Hess (2014) argues that ethics in organisations cannot be built top-down alone. It needs bottom-up input - frontline staff and volunteers see things leadership misses and need voice (a way to raise concerns), recognition (for ethical behaviour, not just performance), and protection (from retaliation when they speak up). It also needs outside-in input - beneficiaries, suppliers, communities, and regulators bring perspectives an internal team will miss.

For a small Irish charity, this maps directly onto the Charities Governance Code: a written whistleblowing policy, a real route for volunteers to raise concerns, and meaningful stakeholder engagement with beneficiaries.

A Practical Example

A small Irish community charity has a multi-year grant for Service A. Demand for Service A is lower than expected. Service B (also run by the charity) is overwhelmed. The temptation: quietly redirect some Service A grant money to fund Service B staff. Both serve the same mission. Nobody is being harmed - or so it seems.

The ethical analysis: the grant is restricted; using it for B without funder approval breaches the grant agreement. Other bidders for the Service A grant did not get it - redirecting the money without consent is unfair to them. Concealing the redirection in reporting is dishonest. The right move: call the funder. Explain what is happening. Ask whether the grant can be flexed. Most funders, asked early and honestly, will help. The same call made after the fact, when the redirection is discovered, ends the relationship.

Common Pitfalls

  • "Everyone does it" - they probably do not, and even if they do, that is not the test
  • Treating ethics training as a tick-box - an online module nobody remembers is not an ethics programme
  • Punishing the messenger - if volunteers raise concerns and nothing happens, your ethical culture is over
  • Confusing legality with ethics - many things are legal but ethically dubious
  • Decisions made alone under pressure - most ethical breaches happen when one person makes a call late and tired

Key Concepts

Responsibility

Own your decisions, their consequences, and your failures. Report problems early; concealing them makes them worse. Know the law that governs your work.

Fairness

Transparent procurement, open recruitment, declared conflicts of interest, clear and visible service allocation criteria. Fairness is also about not playing favourites in the team.

Honesty

Never overstate impact in funder reports. Tell the truth about project status early. Use beneficiary stories only with consent. Restricted funds stay restricted.

Bottom-Up Ethics (Hess 2014)

Ethics built only from the top eventually fails. Frontline staff and volunteers need voice, recognition for ethical behaviour, and protection from retaliation when they raise concerns.
