AI in Project Management

AI doesn't replace methodologies - it supercharges them. For a small Irish charity or business, AI is the most consequential workplace technology since email, and possibly more so. A four-person team that uses AI well can produce more, and to higher standards, than a four-person team that doesn't. The cost is low. The gap is widening.

Three Patterns of AI Use in Project Work

PMI's research (2023) identifies three patterns of GenAI use in project management, organised by complexity and how much human intervention each requires:

  • Automate - low complexity, low intervention. AI does it; you check it. Examples: generating reports, summarising meetings, performing calculations.
  • Assist - medium complexity, some intervention. AI drafts; you shape. Examples: drafting communications, first-pass analyses, research synthesis.
  • Augment - high complexity, high intervention. AI helps you think; you decide. Examples: predictive risk analysis, scenario modelling, strategic decision support.

Houghton's framing of the broader shift: AI takes the science, the human takes the art. The Iron Triangle (scope, time, cost) is increasingly managed by AI. The Human Triangle (leadership, stakeholder negotiation, ethical judgement) is still managed by people - and is probably more important than ever.

What AI Can Do For You Today

For most small Irish organisations, the best place to start is firmly in the Automate and Assist zones. Specific high-value use cases:

  • Drafting - project charters, risk registers, board papers, funder reports, communications, social media, blog posts
  • Summarising - long meetings, dense funder documents, reports, research papers
  • Translating - into plain English, into Irish, between technical and lay registers
  • Brainstorming - lists of risks, stakeholders, ideas to consider
  • Reformatting - turning notes into a structured charter; turning a charter into a presentation
  • Analysing - spotting patterns in feedback, theming open-text survey responses

Five Practical Prompts You Can Use Today

  1. "I'm planning a 6-month community-engagement project funded by Pobal. List the top 10 risks I should consider, organised by Risk Breakdown Structure category (financial, operational, legal, reputational, H&S, technology, stakeholder)."
  2. "Here are my notes from yesterday's project team meeting [paste]. Produce a structured summary with: decisions made, actions assigned (with owner and date), open questions."
  3. "Draft a one-page project charter for this project: [project description]. Use these headings: Sponsor, PM, Objectives, Scope (in/out), Key Deliverables, Budget, Stakeholders, Assumptions, Risks. Plain English. Aimed at a charity board."
  4. "I have to write a quarterly funder report. Here's what we said we'd do [paste], here's what actually happened [paste]. Help me write a 1-page report that's honest about variances but professionally framed."
  5. "Give me 5 questions I should ask each of our key stakeholders [list them] in a discovery interview. Each set tailored to that stakeholder's perspective and likely interests."

The Hard Rules - Non-Negotiable

Never put personal data into a public AI tool

This is the rule that matters most for charities. Public AI tools (free tiers of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) typically retain inputs and may use them to train future models. Beneficiary names, contact details, case notes, and sensitive personal information must never be entered into these tools. GDPR applies here. "I pasted the case notes into ChatGPT to summarise them" is a data breach, and increasingly understood as such by the Data Protection Commission.

If you genuinely need AI on personal data, use a tool with enterprise-grade contracts and a Data Processing Agreement (Microsoft Copilot for M365, ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude for Work).

Always review

AI generates plausible-sounding text that is sometimes wrong - invented citations, fabricated statistics, confident-but-incorrect facts (called hallucinations). For a charity, hallucination in a funder report or board paper is a reputational risk. Treat AI output the way you would treat a willing, fast, slightly unreliable junior colleague: everything is a draft until a human has reviewed it.

Disclose where it matters

For internal drafts and brainstorming, no need to disclose. For public-facing communications and formal reports, say if AI was substantially involved. For academic or evaluation outputs, disclose per the publisher's policy. Do not claim AI work as your own when the writing itself is what is being judged (e.g. funder applications where the writing demonstrates capability).

A Ten-Minute Weekly Team Habit

One person each week picks a real project management task they did manually that week. They redo it with AI and compare - faster? better? worse? Five-minute share at the next team meeting. Three months of this and your team has organic, real-world AI fluency without buying training.

Common Pitfalls

  • Believing the output without checking - hallucinations are common and sometimes elegant
  • Personal data in public tools - a live GDPR risk; get this rule into team norms now
  • Outsourcing judgement - AI can draft a stakeholder analysis; it cannot tell you whether to trust a particular funder's intentions
  • Tool sprawl - picking ten AI tools and using none well; pick one, learn it deeply
  • Underestimating it - "AI is not for us." A small Irish charity that ignores AI will be working much harder than equivalents who did not, within five years

Key Concepts

Three Modes

Automate (AI does, you check), Assist (AI drafts, you shape), Augment (AI helps you think, you decide). Start in Automate and Assist; move to Augment as confidence grows.

GDPR and AI

Never enter beneficiary names, case notes, or sensitive personal data into a public AI tool. This is a data breach. For personal data, use enterprise tools with a Data Processing Agreement.

Hallucination

AI generates plausible but sometimes wrong text: invented citations, fabricated statistics, confident-but-incorrect facts. Treat every AI output as a draft until a human has reviewed it.

AI Fluency Habit

One person per week redoes a manual task with AI and shares the comparison at the next meeting. Three months builds organic team fluency without buying training.
