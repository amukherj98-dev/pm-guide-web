Project Planning & Definition

Most failed projects don't fail at the end. They fail at the start - they were never properly defined. A few hours spent clearly defining a project at the front end saves weeks of confusion later. This is the highest-leverage time you will spend on any project.

The Iron Triangle

Every project has three core constraints (Houghton, 2023, ch.6): Scope (what is being delivered), Time (when it must be delivered), and Cost (what it can cost). The fundamental rule: you can fix any two, but the third must flex. If scope and time are fixed, cost has to absorb the pressure. If cost and time are fixed, scope has to give. There is no way round this.

Houghton's New Iron Triangle adds two further constraints: Quality (is the deliverable fit for purpose?) and Sustainability (will it last? what is the impact on people, planet, and the team?). Use the triangle as a conversation tool. Ask the funder, the board, the team: which of these is fixed for us, which can flex? You will learn more in 20 minutes of that conversation than in 20 pages of project documentation.

The "3 Yes's and a No" Conversation

This is one of Houghton's most useful tools for handling the inevitable mid-project requests for "just one small extra thing." When someone asks for a change, the answer is always yes - three times - before it can become a no:

  1. "Yes, you can have that feature. It will add three days to the timeline - happy to push the launch back?" No, the date is fixed.
  2. "Yes, you can have it on time. We will need to bring in a contractor - can you find €1,500 in the budget?" No, there is no extra money.
  3. "Yes, you can have it within budget and on time. Show me what we should drop from the existing plan." No, everything in there is already essential.
  4. "Then I am afraid the answer is no."

The genius of this approach is that it never says no first. It forces the person asking for the change to understand the trade-off they are requesting. For a small Irish organisation where the project manager often reports to people more senior than them (board members, founders, major funders), this script is invaluable - it is a way of pushing back without confrontation.

Feasibility Investigation

Before committing, ask honestly: should we be doing this at all? Houghton's Feasibility Investigation checks four dimensions:

  • Market - Is there real demand? Who actually wants this? Will beneficiaries use it?
  • Technical - Do we have the skills, tools, and methods to deliver? If not, can we get them?
  • Organisational - Do we have the people, governance, and capacity? Does it fit our charitable purpose?
  • Financial - Can we afford to do it? Can we afford to maintain it after launch?

A clear "no" in any one dimension is a problem worth surfacing now rather than at the halfway mark. These same weaknesses also seed your initial risk identification.

The Business Case

The business case answers: why are we doing this, and is it worth it? For a small organisation it does not need to be long - half a page is often enough. Cover: the problem or opportunity; the proposed response; the benefits (quantitative and qualitative); the costs (money, time, opportunity cost); alternatives considered including "do nothing"; the recommendation and why.

The "do nothing" option is genuinely important. Sometimes the most useful thing a business case can do is establish that the cost of inaction is real.

The Project Charter

The project charter is the one-page agreement that says "this is the project we are doing." Once signed, it is the reference point everyone comes back to when memories diverge. A minimal charter for a small org includes: project name and brief description; project sponsor (one named person ultimately accountable); project manager (one named person running it day-to-day); objectives; scope (what is in, what is out - the "out" list is often more useful); key deliverables and rough milestones; budget envelope; key stakeholders; major assumptions and known risks; sign-off by the sponsor.

Choosing Your Approach

Once the project is defined, choose how to run it: Waterfall (plan everything upfront, sequential), Agile (short iterative cycles), or Hybrid (most small-org projects). See the Methodologies page for the full treatment. The key question: are the requirements stable (lean Waterfall) or will they evolve (lean Agile)?

Common Pitfalls

  • Vague goals - "improve community engagement" is not a project objective; it is a wish. Make objectives specific and testable.
  • Skipping the charter because "everyone knows what we're doing" - six months later, half the team has changed and nobody remembers
  • Scope creep that starts before the project does - "while we're at it, can we also..."
  • Treating the business case as a tick-box for a funder - it is a tool for you, to test whether the project is worth doing
  • Defining the project alone - definition is a conversation; if only one person owns it, you will discover the disagreements during execution

Key Concepts

The Iron Triangle

Scope, Time, and Cost are the three constraints. Fix any two and the third must flex. No project can ignore this; the skill is knowing which two are truly fixed.

3 Yes's and a No

Houghton's script for handling change requests without confrontation. Say yes three times (exploring each trade-off) before saying no. Forces the requester to understand the cost of their ask.

Feasibility Investigation

Four checks before committing: Market (is there demand?), Technical (can we deliver?), Organisational (do we have capacity?), Financial (can we afford to run it after launch?).

Project Charter

The one-page agreement everyone signs before work begins. Sponsor, PM, objectives, scope (in and out), milestones, budget, stakeholders, risks. The reference point when memories diverge.
