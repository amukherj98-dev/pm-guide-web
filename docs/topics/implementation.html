Project Implementation

Implementation is where plans meet reality. The gap between planning and delivery is bridged by practical habits - good check-ins, honest status reporting, clear escalation, and the discipline to close properly at the end. Light templates used consistently beat heavy ones used once.

The Weekly Check-In Habit

A good weekly project check-in is 15 minutes, same time each week, same structure. Traffic-light status (green / amber / red) for each workstream. One-line update per workstream. Anything blocking? New risks this week? Decisions needed? Actions assigned - owner and date. The rule: any red item gets discussed as long as it needs. Green items get one sentence. Amber is when you intervene - do not let it drift to red unnoticed.

The discipline is the structure. An unstructured project meeting that "covers everything" usually covers nothing reliably. The same agenda every week makes it fast and makes gaps visible.

Key Templates for Implementation

Project Charter

The one-page agreement signed before work begins. Project name, sponsor (one named person), PM (one named person), objectives (three to five, specific and testable), scope (in and out - the "out" list is often more useful), key deliverables and milestones, budget envelope, key stakeholders, major assumptions, known risks, sign-off date. Once signed, it is the reference point everyone comes back to when memories diverge.

Risk Register

Risk ID, description, probability (1–5), impact (1–5), score (P × I), stage, detection trigger, effect, response strategy, action, owner, status. A ten-row register reviewed at every meeting is worth ten times a hundred-row register that nobody opens. Review it at every check-in - ask "any new risks?" and "should we re-rate any existing ones?"

Pre-Project Checklist

Before kicking off, check: charter agreed and signed; sponsor identified and committed; PM identified with time allocated; objectives specific and testable; in-scope and out-of-scope written down; top risks identified with owners; methodology chosen; tool chosen and team has access; reporting cadence agreed; decision rights agreed; conflict-of-interest declarations updated.

Project Closure Checklist

Before the team disperses: all deliverables completed or formally varied; final budget reconciled; final funder report submitted; all invoices in and paid; volunteers and staff thanked publicly and personally; retrospective held within two weeks of closure; lessons captured in shared folder; personal data disposed of or migrated per GDPR; files archived per data retention policy; final sign-off by sponsor.

The ECCSR Review

At every lifecycle stage (Initiation, Planning, Execution, Monitoring, Closure), run a five-question check:

  1. Is this project, and the way we are running it, ethical?
  2. Are we genuinely working collaboratively with the people involved?
  3. Are we creating something genuinely useful?
  4. Is what we are doing sustainable - for the team, the environment, and the organisation?
  5. Are we making real progress towards the agreed results?

If any answer is "no" or "not really," that is where to focus next. This five-question check is the practical use of the ECCSR framework as a project management tool. See ECCSR Framework for the full treatment.

Common Implementation Pitfalls

  • Templates filled in once and never reviewed - a risk register written at kick-off and never opened is decoration
  • Status inflation - always green until suddenly red; amber is not a problem, it is information
  • Closure skipped because everyone is busy - the week after a project ends is the one week lessons are fresh; schedule the retrospective in advance
  • Treating the template as the work - the charter is not the project; the risk register is not risk management
  • Personal data not managed at closure - GDPR requires data retention policies to be applied when a project ends

Key Concepts

15-Minute Weekly Check-In

Same time, same structure every week. Traffic-light status, one-line update, blockers, new risks, decisions, actions with owners. Red gets full attention; green gets one sentence.

Project Charter

One page, signed before work begins. Sponsor, PM, objectives, in/out scope, milestones, budget, stakeholders, risks, sign-off. The reference point when memories diverge.

ECCSR Review

Five questions at every lifecycle stage: ethical? collaborative? creating something useful? sustainable? delivering real results? Any "no" is where to focus next.

Proper Project Closure

Confirm deliverables, reconcile budget, submit final report, thank contributors, run retrospective, capture lessons, archive files, manage personal data per GDPR. A project that ends without closure wastes its own experience.
