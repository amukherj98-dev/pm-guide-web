ECCSR Framework

Sustainable

The S in ECCSR. Sustainability in project management has three intertwined meanings: environmental (what is the carbon, energy, waste impact?), social (are we treating people well?), and operational (can the result be maintained after the project ends?). For a charity, the third meaning is often the killer.

Three Meanings of Sustainable

Houghton's book treats sustainability as core to project management, not an optional add-on. The S in ECCSR covers three dimensions simultaneously:

  • Environmental sustainability - what is the carbon, energy, waste, and materials impact of this project?
  • Social sustainability - are we treating staff, volunteers, beneficiaries, and community well in the process?
  • Operational sustainability - can the result of this project be maintained after it ends?

The GPM P5 Standard extends this to five dimensions (People, Planet, Prosperity, Process, Product) - see the Sustainability page for the full treatment. The ECCSR framework's S dimension points to all five.

Environmental Sustainability in Practice

Small organisations do not need a carbon audit. The discipline is to ask the question: What is the environmental footprint of this project? Practical actions: paperless events, local suppliers, hybrid meetings to reduce travel, reusable signage, refurbished equipment over new. Each decision is small - compounded over many projects, the effect is real.

Social Sustainability - The Team

A project that delivers on time but ends with the volunteer coordinator on sick leave has not really succeeded. Social sustainability inside the project means: realistic workloads, buffer in plans, psychological safety for raising concerns, recognition that is genuine rather than perfunctory, and no sole points of failure where one person's departure would collapse the project.

Operational Sustainability - The Cliff Edge

The most common failure mode for Irish charity projects: a grant-funded service that vanishes when the grant ends. Beneficiaries who built relationships with staff lose them. Trust takes a hit. A sustainable project is one that has a credible plan for what comes after the funding - built into the project from the start, not scrambled for in the final six months.

Three honest options: mainstream (the work becomes part of core operations), re-fund (a credible follow-on pathway exists and has been identified), or wind down with care (the project has a defined end, and beneficiaries are transitioned thoughtfully to alternatives). All three are legitimate. The failure is having no plan.

Sustainability Across the Project Lifecycle

At Initiation: Is it sustainable in all three senses? At Planning: Are sustainability concerns built in, not bolted on? At Execution: Are we burning out the team? At Monitoring: Are sustainability indicators tracked? At Closure: Will it last - and is the answer honest?

Key Concepts

Three Sustainability Dimensions

Environmental (carbon, energy, waste), Social (treating people well through the process), Operational (can the result be maintained after the project ends?). All three matter equally.

The Cliff Edge

Grant-funded services that vanish when funding ends. Beneficiaries build relationships and then lose them. Plan for what comes after the funding from the project definition stage, not the final report.

Team Sustainability

Realistic workloads, buffer in plans, no sole points of failure, psychological safety. A project that broke its volunteer coordinator has not succeeded, regardless of what it delivered.

Three Post-Project Options

Mainstream the work, re-fund it, or wind down with care. All three are legitimate. The failure is having no plan when the funding ends.
